Replicating Chain Ladder

Replicating Chain Ladder An Overview

Stochastic Models Replicating Chain Ladder (2 approaches)

  • Mack Chain Ladder (Modelling claim development)
    • Assumption: \(E\left[C_{i, j} \mid \mathcal{F}_{j-1}\right]=\lambda_{j} C_{i ,j-1}\)
      • \(j=1,\dots,9\)
    • Different to: \(E\left[C_{i, j} \mid \mathcal{F}_{j-1}\right]=\lambda_{j} E[C_{i ,j-1}]\) and stronger than: \(E\left[C_{i, j}\right]=\lambda_{j} E[C_{i ,j-1}]\)
    • Implication: Payments in early developments affect all later payments
    • See Mack (1993), Mack (1994)
  • GLM based methods (Modelling Claim amount)
    • Assumption: \(E\left[X_{i, j}\right]=\alpha_i\beta_j\)
      • \(i,j=1,\dots,10\)
    • Implication:
      • Increments \(X_{ij}\) are independent
      • development effect and accident effect are independent.
    • See (among many others) Kremer (1982), Mack (1991), Renshaw and Verrall (1998)

Replicating Chain Ladder An Overview

Mack (1994) and Mack and Venter (2000) point out that the Mack model is different to modelling the Claim amount.

Mack and Venter (2000)

  • The chain ladder algorithm does not compute any parameters other than the development factors.
  • The parameters \(\alpha_i\) of GLM have an equivalent neither in the chain ladder algorithm nor in the Mack model.
  • The fact that the true expected reserves are different, clearly shows that the reserve estimators of both models coincide only incidentally and not due to any inner connection.



Granular Reserving methods

Granular Reserving Methods An Overview

The development of a claim

Arjas (1989), Norberg (1993) propose to model claim arrival according to a non-homogeneous Poisson process, \(N\), with intensity \(w(t),\) meaning that \[\Pr(N(t)=k)={\frac {\left(\int_0^t w(s) \mathrm ds\right)^{k}}{k!}}e^{-\int_0^t w(s) \mathrm ds}.\] - \(w(t)\) is some measure of volume or size of the business


This is new

Exposure is needed as additional data to estimate claim development

Granular Reserving Methods Without Exposure?

Question

Is it possible to develop a granular reserving framework without modelling exposure? (Chain ladder does not need exposure data…GLM estimates \(\alpha_i\))

Why interesting?

  • Less strict data requirements
  • Alternative estimator
  • Can be used to enhance prediction (model selection or ensembles)



Why doesn’t chain ladder need exposure?

Why doesn’t chain ladder need exposure?

  • Chain Ladder estimates development factors
    • development factors are used to extrapolate via a chain principle
  • This is similar to the idea that the survival function is the product of hazard functions
    • \(S(t)= \prod_0^t (1-dA(s))\)

Why doesn’t chain ladder need exposure?

  • By comparing the current cell with the previous cells, chain ladder is avoiding the need of extra exposure data.

  • Technically: By reversing the development time right truncation is transformed to left truncation. \[T_i < \mathcal T - Y_i \Leftrightarrow T_i^R > Y_i, \quad T_i^R= \mathcal T - T_i\]

Left Truncation

Given a survival time \(T\) and a truncation variable \(R\), we say that \(T\) is left truncated if we only have observations \((T_i,R_i)\) if \[T_i> R_i.\]

Why doesn’t chain ladder need exposure?

Hiabu (2017) and Bischofberger, Hiabu, and Isakson (2020) discuss

  • Chain ladder’s development factor are the discrete equivalent to a hazard function running in reversed development time.
  • One can use survival analysis techniques to estimate the hazard function
    • Utilizing methods from Biostatistics to incorporate (time dependent) covariates
    • Including more recent machine learning methods for survival data


But no practical implementation … until now …

Chain Ladder Plus: Setting

Chain Ladder Plus Setting

Run-off triangle (Incremental payments): \[X^{(m)} =\left\{X_{k j}: k,j=0, \ldots, m;k+j \leq m \right\}\] stems from i.i.d. payments \((Z_i,U_i,T_i), i=1,\dots,n\):

  • \(Z_i\) is the payment size.
  • \(U_i\) accident date.
  • \(T_i\) the delay between accident and payment.

Chain Ladder Plus Setting

We want to estimate \[\alpha^{\ast,R}(t|u)= \frac{ E[Z_1 | T_1=t, U_1=u]}{ E\left[Z_1 | \ T_1 \leq t, U_1=u\right]} \underbrace{ \lim_{h \downarrow 0} h^{-1}{P}\left(T_i\in(t-h,t]|\ T_i \leq t < T-U_i, \ U_i=u\right)}_{\alpha^R(t|u)}\]

  • \({\alpha^R(t|u)}=\alpha(t^R|u)= \lim_{h \downarrow 0} h^{-1}{P}\left(T_i^R\in[t,t_h)|\ T_i^R \geq t > U_i, \ U_i=u\right)\)

  • \(T_i^R= \mathcal T-T_i, \ t^R=\mathcal T-t\)

Chain Ladder Plus Setting

  • We only have information aggregated in the run-off triangle.
  • Hence we can only estimate some averaged version


We propose to estimate \[\mu_{kj}=\frac{\delta \int_{\mathcal{P}_{kj}} \alpha^{\ast,R}(s|u)p_U(u)\gamma(s,u) ds du} {\int_{\mathcal{P}_{kj}} p_U(u)\gamma(s,u) ds}\]

  • \(\gamma(s,u)=E[Y_i^{\ast,R} | U_i=u]\)
  • \(p_U\) is the marginal density of \(U_i\).

\(\phantom{,}\)

Aggregation in Parallelograms

\[\mathcal{P}_{kj}= \{(t,u): t_j+u_k-u\leq t \leq t_{j+1}+u_k-u; u\in [u_k,u_{k+1} ), t\geq 0\},\] i.e., the individual observations \((Z_i,T_i,U_i), i=1,\dots n\) are compressed into observations \(X_{kj};\ j,k=0,\dots,m\) with

\[X_{kj}= \sum_i Z_i \int I\left((s,U_i)\in \mathcal{P}_{kj}\right) dN_i(s).\]

Chain Ladder Plus Setting

If observations on the individual level would be available, we could estimate \[\mu_{kj}=\frac{\delta \int_{{\color{#5692e4}\mathcal{P}_{kj}}}{\color{#5692e4} p_U(u) }{\color{#D9421A}\gamma(s,u)\alpha^{\ast,R}(s|u)}ds du} {\int_{\mathcal{P}_{kj}} p_U(u){\color{#009151}\gamma(s,u)} ds}\] via

\[\begin{align*} & \qquad \frac{\sum_i {\color{#D9421A}Z_i}\int {\color{#5692e4}I\left((s,U_i)\in \mathcal{P}_{kj}\right)}{\color{#D9421A} dN_i}(s)} {\delta^{-1}\sum_i\int I\left((s,U_i)\in \mathcal{P}_{kj}\right) {\color{#009151}Y_i^{\ast,R}(s)} ds}\\[10pt] &=\frac{X_{kj}} {\delta^{-1}\sum_i\int I\left((s,U_i)\in \mathcal{P}_{kj}\right) Y_i^{\ast,R}(s) ds}\\[10pt] &=\frac{X_{kj}} {\sum_{l<j} X_{kl}+ \delta^{-1}\sum_{i: I\left((T_i,U_i)\in \mathcal{P}_{kj}\right)=1} \int I\left((s,U_i)\in \mathcal{P}_{kj}\right) Y_i^{\ast,R}(s) ds}. \end{align*}\]

We need to approximate \(\delta^{-1}\sum_{i: I\left((T_i,U_i)\in \mathcal{P}_{kj}\right)=1} \int I\left((s,U_i)\in \mathcal{P}_{kj}\right) Y_i^{\ast,R}(s) ds\)

Chain Ladder Plus Setting

Lemma

Assume \((T,U)\) is uniformly distributed if conditioned on the parallelogram \(\mathcal{P}_{kj}\), then \[E\left[\delta^{-1}\sum_{i: I\left((T_i,U_i)\in \mathcal{P}_{kj}\right)=1} \int I\left((s,U_i)\in \mathcal{P}_{kj}\right) Y_i^{\ast,R}(s) ds\right]=0.5 X_{kj}.\]

Proof

For claims in the pralleogram \(k,j\) the length of the crossing from entering the parallelogram until payment in expected terms is equal to \(0.5\delta:\)

\[\begin{align*} \delta^{-2}\int_0^{\mathcal T} \int_0^{\mathcal T} I\left((s,u)\in \mathcal{P}_{kj}\right) \left(t_{j+1}-t-u+u_k \right)\ ds du = \frac{1}{2} \delta \end{align*}\]

Chain Ladder Plus Setting

Hence, we propose to estimate \(\mu_{kj}\) via \[\widehat \mu_{kj}=\frac{X_{kj}} {\sum_{l<j} X_{kl}+ \frac 1 2 X_{kj}}=\frac{X_{kj}}{E_{kj}}.\]

Getting development factors

For \(k=0,\dots,m; j=1,\dots m\), chain ladder’s individual development factors are given by \[\widehat f_{kj}=\frac{\sum_{l\leq j}X_{kj}} {\sum_{l<j} X_{kl}},\] such that \[\widehat f_{kj}= \frac{2+\widehat \mu_{kj}}{2-\widehat \mu_{kj}}.\]



Chain Ladder Plus: A stochastic model replicating chain ladder

Chain Ladder Plus A stochastic model replicating chain ladder

Assume: \[\log(\mu_{kj})= a_j, \qquad [\text{age-model}]\] under a Poisson assumption for \(X_{kj}\) given \(E_{kj} \ (j\geq 1)\):

\[X_{kj}|E_{kj} \sim \text{Pois}(O_{kj}), \quad O_{kj}:= E_{kj} \mu_{kj}.\]

Proposition

\[\begin{align*} \hat f^{\texttt{clmplus(age)}}_j=\frac{2+ exp( \hat a_j)}{2- exp( \hat a_j)}=\frac{ \sum_{l\leq j}\sum_{k=0}^{m-j} X_{k , l}}{ \sum_{l< j}\sum_{k=0}^{m-j} X_{k , l}}= \hat f^{\text{clm}}_j. \end{align*}\]

Final Remarks

Conclusion

  • We introduced a framework to model the claims development via a GLM.

  • This is in contrast to existing GLM based reserving literature which models the claim amount.

  • Modeling the claim development via an age model replicates chain ladder.

  • Simplicity of the model invites very naturally to consider model extensions.

  • In the data sets considered, including additional effects such as calendar effects and cohort effects often improved the fit.

  • But there are exceptions and our data study demonstrated that the best results are obtained by having both claim amount models and claim development models at one’s disposal.
